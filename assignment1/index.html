Q1

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The rapid firing bold text of the loading screen was the first thing I paid attention to. Then to the cascading movement of the individual elements over the video background once the loading is complete.

Q2

Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  1. Tracking new custom cursor shape
  2. Watch video in the background
  3. Look at interactive elements responding to the moving cursor
  4. Attempt to click to the side on the flat arrow like icon. Non responsive action
  5. Scroll wheel, watch the boxed texts scroll alongside the screen.
  6. Scrolling down to the next page. Watch the screen goes from vertical to horizontal movement
  7. Click on “Villainy” Page
  8. Click on 'R' - back to Home
  9. Click on 'B' - Rick Brown information

Q3

What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

Scrolling through each section of the websites to navigate, then scrolling back and forth to see how the boxes, text elements and images tilt and morph opposite, or perpendicular to the scroll direction.

Q4

What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

Hovering before clicking on elements to see how each would react, if it would react at all, then clicking out of the page to return to the main menu. Hovering more so than clicking, as it had more interactions built in and encompass mouse movement interactions as a whole.
Examples: Image tilt in 3D, color background change, hover pops up.

Q5

What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The primary goal is for showcasing the talent and out of the box, modern graphical design capability of the web designer, through layout and programming interactions. Separate it from standard websites, meaning to hit hard with first impression. Extremely user friendly in terms of interactions, but not accommodating to all users.

Q6

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

Since the loading screen, an overload amounts to interactive or moving elements, including the cursor itself. Flashy approach to the design, with everything made to be attention grabbing. Even the typography was used as a design element to be interacted with, over an informative one.
Especially done with modern technology in mind, with an overload of moving elements that would require some level of fast internet and computing power.
The lack of accessibility in menu, headings in html, font size, meant the website is not built for general access.

Q7

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

The experience should be quick and snappy, a quick impressive look through, with pausing on each new novel interactive elements, but ultimately not for long, or recurring visits.

Q8

How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

Using only one way to navigate which is by scrolling, with no comprehensive menu, it created a one way road experience, with little branching or back and forth. And by making almost everything interactable, it made everything attention grabbing, which is good for first impressions. The lack of clear menu UI made it difficult to quickly skip to specific contents, and became a hassle to navigate once the novelty wore off.
All additional sites are available on one single plus sign element that is always available, meaning navigating out to check other sites of the designer is significantly more accessible.

Q9

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

  • A heavy focus on contemporary graphic design with vector shapes reminds me of posters and brochures.
  • Cinematic shots at the beginning, in the video background, and in reel preview as well, seemed to have taken inspiration in cinematic shots, or documentary, with slow walking and close shots.
  • Modern, high tech interface, with a little pretentious presentation, evoke high end products.
  • Extremely attention demanding, which evoke advertisements for the designer, or more recent examples, Gacha games, where the purpose is to hold the user's attention and reward it with eye-catching, popping visuals as much as possible.

Q10

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

Be dazzled scrolling through the movements of every element, but not necessarily lingering on any texts. Absorb the experience of interacting with and navigating a website as an experience itself. With its complexity in presentation. Linger on visual/graphical elements, but not for long.
Check out resources/additional items within it to see other products done by the same entity, and move to purchase when sufficiently impressed.
These are functions of good advertisement, the window displays of high end stores or eye-catching posters of concerts. Names and important indentify information, branding are bold. The information for payment processing or commission details are often elsewhere, not to deter customers but seen when they are about to buy in.

Q11

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

Fascination, excitement over discovery of a new interaction (ie: tilt, mouse track circle expand) High energy. Wanting to see what's next constantly, and moving on from the last.
This showed a focus on presentation, on being eye catching, an interactive art piece over a clear and concise portfolio meant for information. Which worked well as a piece of advertisement, or a way to promote the portfolio of the designer himself. But also the modern trends of being more technical advance, higher energy, less attention span.

Q12

What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The lack of obvious, clear basic UI elements, such as menu bar, making it disorienting. This meant how to navigate the page was unclear at the start, and annoying on repeating visit.
Biggest problem is the overt use of moving elements. Especially on the already small display fonts, making it even harder to read. Every image tilts when scroll, color jumbles when mouse hover. More images shown on mouse hover over text. The background video is moving. Everything demands attention, which muddles the focal point. Long experience creates motion sickness.

Q13

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

Innovative use of website layouts. Better to understand as a graphical piece than an actual informative piece. Typography is hard to read but becomes interesting once understood as more a graphical element, though it still, even if overlooked, carries its uses as information as well.
Layers of interaction, from the mouse to movement of elements in relation to mouse position, velocity, scroll speed and scroll direction. It feels as though the designer wanted interactions, and doubled down on nailing it.
